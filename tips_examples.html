Tips and examples to improve communication with your dependant/s

The following information will provide you with tips and advice that will assist you in communicating with your dependant in an honest and age appropriate manner. The information presented to you in this section should not be a substitute for professional medical or personal advice. The government understands that responsible adults know their children best and this information should be used as a guide to your dependants needs.

Infant (0 - 1)

Infants rely heavily on their responsible adult and those around them. It is imperative that you are looking after your own mental health as they will notice a change in your routine and mood (Emerging Minds, 2020). This time as a responsible adult can be highly stressful, ensure you are leaning on those around you for support.

Changes in routine can be noticed by your dependant, this can include;

  • How you are feeling.
  • How distracted you are and an increase of the amount of time you are on your device.
  • How you are responding to them.

    • Above tips from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

You can support your dependants during the COVID-19 pandemic by;

  • Maintaining your routine and ensuring you are spending regular time together.
  • Try to limit having the television on when your dependent is in the room.
  • Ensure that you are spending quality time with your dependant with no distractions.
  • Find a way to keep connected to those you love through video calls and creating artwork with your dependant for those individuals.
  • Ensure you are taking time out for your mental health.

    • Above tips from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

Toddler (1 - 3)

With a dependant in this age bracket it is important that you are using simple language and providing examples in a way that your dependant will understand (Beyond Blue, 2020). This may include telling your dependant that COVID-19 is caused by a new germ or bug which makes people sick (Australian Red Cross, 2020). Ensure that you are looking after yourself and your mental health during this time.

You can support your toddler during the COVID-19 pandemic by;

  • Maintaining your routine and ensuring you are spending regular time together.
  • Try to limit having the television on when your dependent is in the room.
  • Ensure that you are spending quality time with your dependant with no distractions.
  • Find a way to keep connected to those you love through video calls and creating artwork with your dependant for those individuals.
  • Ensure you are taking time out for your mental health.

    • Above tips from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

When talking about COVID-19 with your toddler, it is important to discuss these key areas;

  • Most dependants will not get very sick from COVID-19. But if they do it will be a bit like a flu or cold.
  • Reassure your dependant that there are Doctors and Nurses who are working hard to take care of those who are sick.
  • COVID-19 is spread through close contact with someone who has caught the germ. This germ can get on their hands and be put in their body by touching their face and nose. This means that you should try to discourage your dependant from touching their face without washing their hands.
  • What might happen if your dependant gets sick. These symptoms can include (but are not limited to) a fever, a dry cough and tiredness (NSW Government, 2020).

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

To keep your toddler safe, you can teach them to;

  • Wash their hands with soap and water whilst singing “Happy Birthday” to keep the bad germs away.
  • Cover their coughs and sneezes with a tissue or with the bend of their elbow.
  • Keep a distance away from other people.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

When discussing COVID-19 with your toddler it is important to listen to their concerns and be reassuring. If you are unable to answer your toddlers questions be honest and try to seek the answers you need (Victorian Government, 2020).

Preschooler (3 - 5)

When discussing COVID-19 with dependants in this age bracket, it is important that you are honest and use language that your dependant will understand (Beyond Blue, 2020). You can do this by comparing the COVID-19 virus to a normal cold or flu. However, it is important to stress that it can be more serious to those who may be older. When discussing COVID-19, your preschooler may have questions. Listen to your dependants concerns and offer empathy and understanding (Bright Horizons, 2020). If you are unable to answer some of your dependants questions, be honest and try to seek the information (Victorian Government, 2020). Ensure that you are using a calm and reassuring perspective to reduce feelings of stress and anxiety in your dependant (Bright Horizons, 2020).

When talking about COVID-19 with your Preschooler, it is important to discuss these key areas;

  • Most dependants will not get very sick from COVID-19. But if they do it may be a little bit like a cold or flu.
  • COVID-19 is spread through close contact with someone who has caught the germ. The germ can be spread to your dependant by touching different surfaces then touching their face without washing their hands.
  • The symptoms of COVID-19 can be like the common cold. These include (but are not limited to) a fever, a dry cough and tiredness (link to symptoms NSW health).

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

To keep your preschooler safe by teaching them to;

  • Wash their hands with soap and water whilst singing “Happy Birthday” to keep the bad germs away.
  • Carry hand sanitiser and use it to cleanse their hands when there is no water available.
  • Cover their coughs and sneezes with a tissue or with the bend of their elbow.
  • Keep a distance away from other people.
  • Eat healthy food.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

Middle Childhood (6 - 11)

Like adults, dependants in middle childhood are also exposed to a large amount of information and often it is difficult to sort through fact, fiction and what is unknown (Emerging Minds, 2020). Dependants in Middle Childhood can often be confused by the information they have found or heard from various sources often, they will come to you with clarifying questions or to help them understand what is happening (Emerging Minds, 2020). It is ok to not have the answers to their questions. Take this as an opportunity to find the information out together (Victorian Government, 2020).

It is essential that when discussing COVID-19 with your dependant you understand their worries and are able to share accurate and age appropriate information. For this reason you should actively seek reliable information to help you to respond to your dependants questions. You can do this by choosing organisation websites such as the World Health Organisation (WHO) and the Australian Department of health.

Before you discuss COVID-19 with your dependant you should;

  • Ensure you are aware of your feelings as dependants use you as a guide for how they should be feeling.
  • Try to relax and stay calm so you can offer a reassuring perspective to your dependant.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

  • Try to limit your dependants exposure to multiple sources of news and media as this can be overwhelming for yourself and your dependant.

    • Above tip from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

You can start discussing COVID-19 with your dependant by;

  • Beginning the conversation gently, allowing your dependant to talk and follow their lead.

    • Above tip from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

  • Asking your dependant what they already know and clear up any confusion of bias that your dependant may be showing.

    • Above tip from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

When talking about COVID-19 with your dependant in Middle Childhood, it is important to;

  • Discuss that there is a risk of your dependant contracting the virus, but reassure them that it will be like a cold or flu.
  • Reassure your dependant that there are Doctors and Nurses who are working hard to take care of those who are sick.
  • Discuss that COVID-19 is spread through close contact with someone who has the virus, through droplets in the air (from sneezing and coughing) and through touching surfaces infected with the virus.
  • Discuss the symptoms of the virus and how it can appear like the common cold. These symptoms include (but are not limited to) a fever, a dry cough and tiredness.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

  • Allow the conversation to continue and reassure your dependant that you will try to answer any questions that they have in the future.

    • Above tip from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

You can keep your Middle Childhood dependant safe by teaching them to

  • Wash their hands with soap and water for 20 seconds to keep their hands healthy.
  • Carry hand sanitiser and use it to cleanse their hands when there is no water available.
  • Cover their coughs and sneezes with a tissue or with the bend of their elbow.
  • Keep 1.5m away from other people.
  • Eat Healthy food and exercise regularly.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

Young Teen (12 - 14)

Young Teens are active on social media and are able to actively seek their own answers to information on the internet. However, this age bracket may require assistance sorting through fact, fiction and what is unknown (Emerging Minds, 2020). Young teens may come to you as their responsible adult to clarify and discuss what they have heard or read through various sources (Emerging Minds, 2020). It is ok to not have the answers to your Young Teens questions, take this as an opportunity to find the correct answer together (Victorian Government, 2020). Use a calm and reassuring tone as the changes that the pandemic has caused can lead to increased stress and anxiety (Bright Horizons, 2020). It is important to listen to your Young Teens concerns and offer empathy and understanding (Bright Horizons, 2020).

It is essential that when discussing COVID-19 with your dependant you understand their worries and are able to share accurate and age appropriate information. For this reason you should actively seek reliable information to help you to respond to your dependants questions. You can do this by choosing organisation websites such as the World Health Organisation (WHO) and the Australian Department of health.

You can start discussing COVID-19 with your dependant by;

  • Beginning the conversation gently, allowing your dependant to talk and follow their lead.

    • Above tip from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

  • Asking your dependant what they already know about COVID-19, physical distancing and self isolation.

    • Above tip from (Raising Children, 2020)

  • Take the opportunity to clear up any confusion of bias that your dependant may be showing.

    • Above tip from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

When discussing COVID-19 with your Young Teen it is important to;

  • Talk about the media coverage and how they are feeling about the pandemic.
  • Explain physical distancing and self isolation.

    • Above tip from (Raising Children, 2020)

  • Discuss that there is a risk of your Young Teen contracting COVID-19, but reassure them that it will be like a bad cold or flu.
  • Reassure your dependant that there are medical staff who are working hard to take care of those who are sick. There are also researchers trying to understand the virus and are working towards a vaccine.
  • Discuss that the virus is spread through close contact with individuals, droplets in the air and touching infected surfaces.
  • Discuss the symptoms of the virus and how it can appear like the common cold. These symptoms include (but are not limited to) a fever, a dry cough and tiredness.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

  • Allow the conversation to continue and reassure your dependant that you will try to answer their questions that they have in the future.

    • Above tip from (Emerging Minds, 2020)

To keep your dependant safe remind them to;

  • Wash their hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds to clean their hands from any virus.
  • Carry hand sanitiser and use it to cleanse their hands when soap and water is not available.
  • Cough and sneeze into a tissue or the bend or their elbow.
  • Keep 1.5m distance from other people.
  • Look after themselves through exercise, a healthy diet and adequate sleep.

    • Above tips from (Bright Horizons, 2020)

Teenager (15 - 17)

Teenagers are extremely active on social media and the internet. This may lead your dependant to gather their own information and form their own opinions based on what they have read. For this reason, your role as a responsible adult is to encourage your dependant to be critical of the information they have heard and read on the internet (Emerging Minds, 2020). Your Teenager may have difficulty sorting through fact, fiction and what is unknown (Emerging Minds, 2020). It is important that you attempt to understand how your dependant is feeling as the pandemic may have caused an increased sense of stress or anxiety. Use a reassuring tone and offer empathy and understanding where possible (Bright Horizons, 2020). It is equally important that you listen to and attempt to answer any questions that your Teen may have. It is ok to not have all the answers, you can take this opportunity to try and find the answer together (Victorian Government, 2020).
